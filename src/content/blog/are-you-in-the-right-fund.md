Here's a stat that should bother you: **73% of Kiwis have never reviewed their KiwiSaver fund type.** Not once.

That means most people are investing their retirement savings based on a decision they made (or that was made for them) years ago — and never looked at again.

Your fund type is the single biggest factor in how much your KiwiSaver grows over time. Getting it wrong could cost you over $200,000 by retirement. So let's break it down.

## The five fund types explained

### Defensive / Cash

- **Risk level**: Very low
- **Expected returns**: Low (roughly 2-4% per year)
- **Best for**: People about to retire or withdraw soon

These funds invest mostly in cash and fixed-interest assets. Your balance won't swing around much, but it also won't grow much. Think of it as a savings account with extra steps.

### Conservative

- **Risk level**: Low
- **Expected returns**: Low-moderate (roughly 3-5% per year)
- **Best for**: People within 5 years of retirement

A step up from defensive. Mostly bonds and cash, with a small slice of shares. Smoother ride, but limited growth.

### Balanced

- **Risk level**: Medium
- **Expected returns**: Moderate (roughly 5-7% per year)
- **Best for**: People 10-15 years from retirement, or those who want a middle ground

A 50/50 split between growth assets (shares, property) and income assets (bonds, cash). The "hedge your bets" option.

### Growth

- **Risk level**: Medium-high
- **Expected returns**: Higher (roughly 7-9% per year)
- **Best for**: People with 15+ years until retirement

Mostly invested in shares and property. You'll see bigger ups and downs year to year, but over the long run, growth funds have historically delivered significantly better returns.

### Aggressive / High Growth

- **Risk level**: High
- **Expected returns**: Highest (roughly 8-10%+ per year)
- **Best for**: Young people with decades until retirement

Almost entirely in shares. Maximum growth potential, maximum volatility. If you're in your 20s or 30s, this is often where you should be — but most people aren't, because they either chose conservative by default or never changed from whatever they were assigned.

## The big mistake: being too conservative too young

This is the single most common KiwiSaver mistake in New Zealand.

If you're 25 and in a conservative fund, you've got 40 years until retirement. That's 40 years of compounding where you're earning 3-4% instead of 7-9%.

Run the numbers and the difference is staggering. On a $50,000 balance with regular contributions, over 40 years that could be a **$200,000+ difference** in your retirement savings.

Why does this happen? Usually one of three reasons:

1. **You were assigned a default fund** when you joined KiwiSaver and never changed it
2. **You chose conservative because it sounded "safe"** — but safe in the short term can be expensive in the long term
3. **Nobody told you** that fund type matters this much

## The other mistake: being too aggressive too late

On the flip side, if you're 60 and in an aggressive growth fund, a bad year in the markets could wipe out years of gains right when you're about to need the money.

As you approach retirement, it makes sense to gradually shift towards more conservative investments. Not all at once — but steadily, so you're not exposed to a big crash when you can least afford it.

## How to check your fund type

1. **Log in to your provider's website** — your fund type should be displayed on your dashboard
2. **Check your last annual statement** — it'll list your fund name and type
3. **Call your provider** — they can tell you in 2 minutes

## So what should you do?

The right fund type depends on:

- **Your age** — younger = more time to recover from market dips
- **When you need the money** — retirement, first home, or something else
- **Your risk tolerance** — how comfortable you are with your balance going up and down
- **Your other savings** — if KiwiSaver is your only retirement saving, fund choice matters even more

There's no one-size-fits-all answer. But there's definitely a wrong answer for most people, and it's usually "the default fund I was put in 10 years ago and never changed."

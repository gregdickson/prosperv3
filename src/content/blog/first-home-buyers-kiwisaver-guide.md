Buying your first home is probably the biggest financial move you'll ever make. And your KiwiSaver? It could be a secret weapon — if you know how to use it properly.

The problem is, the rules are confusing, the information online is scattered, and most people don't find out what they're entitled to until it's too late.

So here's everything in one place.

## Can you use KiwiSaver to buy a first home?

Yes. If you've been a KiwiSaver member for at least 3 years, you can withdraw most of your balance to put towards your first home purchase.

There are a few conditions:

- You must be a **first home buyer** (never owned property before, with some exceptions)
- You must have been a KiwiSaver member for **at least 3 years**
- You need to keep a minimum of **$1,000** in your account
- The property must be your **primary residence** (not an investment property)

## The HomeStart Grant — free money most people forget about

The Government's HomeStart Grant gives you up to **$5,000** (or $10,000 if buying a new build) towards your first home. If you're buying as a couple, that's up to **$20,000** between you.

### Eligibility

- Been a KiwiSaver member for 3+ years
- Income under $95,000 (individual) or $150,000 (couple)
- Property price within regional caps (varies by area)

### How much you get

- **Existing home**: $1,000 for each year of KiwiSaver membership (up to 5 years = $5,000)
- **New build**: $2,000 for each year of membership (up to 5 years = $10,000)

This is literally free money. And a shocking number of eligible Kiwis don't claim it.

## Getting your fund type right for your timeline

This is where a lot of first home buyers get tripped up.

If you're buying in the **next 1-2 years**, you probably want to be in a **conservative or balanced fund**. Why? Because a market dip right before your purchase could slash your deposit.

If you're buying in **5+ years**, you might be better off in a **growth fund** — more time to ride out the ups and downs, and more growth potential for your deposit.

The worst thing you can do? Leave it in whatever default fund you were assigned years ago without checking if it still makes sense.

## Contribution strategy

The standard employee contribution rate is 3%, but you can choose 4%, 6%, 8%, or 10%.

If you're saving for a first home, bumping up your contribution rate means:
- More going into your deposit pot every payday
- More employer match (your employer matches at least 3%)
- Potentially more government contributions (up to $521.43/year)

The trade-off is less take-home pay, so it's about finding the balance that works for your budget.

## Common mistakes to avoid

**Mistake 1: Not knowing what you can withdraw.** A lot of first home buyers underestimate how much KiwiSaver they can actually pull out.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting the HomeStart grant.** Seriously, check your eligibility. It's free money.

**Mistake 3: Being in the wrong fund type for your timeline.** If you're buying next year and you're in an aggressive growth fund, you're gambling with your deposit.

**Mistake 4: Not checking your PIR tax rate.** If your PIR is set wrong, you might be overpaying tax on your KiwiSaver returns — money that could go towards your house.

**Mistake 5: Going it alone.** The rules are complex and change regularly. Getting independent advice can make a real difference to how much you end up with.
